You might think that the formal economy is the backbone of the DRC, but the reality is quite different. After analyzing the economic data, it turns out that a staggering 90% of the active workforce operates in the informal sector, leaving only 10% in formal employment.

But nothing compares to the scale of the informal economy in the DRC. Let’s explore what makes this sector so vast and how it impacts the overall economy.

I recently noticed that during discussions about economic growth, someone will inevitably mention the informal sector, often saying, “Oh, but you have to consider the informal workers; they are the backbone of the economy” (which is almost true).

But not everyone understands the full picture. Since the informal economy is only as strong as its contributions, I delved into the available data to substantiate that claim. So, what makes the informal economy in the DRC so significant?

When you break it down to the numbers, the informal sector contributes a substantial portion to the GDP, despite being largely unrecognized. The GDP per capita may seem misleading at $697, but this figure masks the stark disparities in wealth distribution.

This gives us a clearer view of the economic landscape in the DRC. The larger the segment of the population engaged in informal work, the more pronounced the economic inequality becomes.
